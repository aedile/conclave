## Overview

This report documents the quality degradation curves for the Air-Gapped Synthetic Data Engine's
Differential Privacy CTGAN implementation at varying epsilon levels. Vanilla (non-DP) CTGAN is
used as the baseline.

Phase 30 replaced the proxy-model approach with discriminator-level DP-SGD (ADR-0036). The
benchmark now tests three configurations:

1. **Vanilla CTGAN** — no DP; quality baseline.
2. **Discriminator-level DP** (Phase 30 primary) — Opacus DP-SGD applied directly to the
   `OpacusCompatibleDiscriminator`. Epsilon accounting reflects real Discriminator gradient
   steps on real training data. This is the only configuration with a mathematically rigorous
   end-to-end DP guarantee.
3. **Proxy-model DP** (historical) — Opacus DP-SGD applied to a proxy linear model
   (`_activate_opacus_proxy`, the renamed T7.3 fallback). Epsilon accounting reflects gradient
   steps on a linear proxy model — not the CTGAN Discriminator. Retained for historical
   comparison with pre-Phase-30 results.

> **Phase 30 implementation note**: Discriminator-level epsilon values are the authoritative DP
> measurement for Phase 30+ deployments. The proxy-model section is retained for historical
> comparison only. See ADR-0036 for the discriminator-level approach and ADR-0025 for the
> historical proxy-model rationale.

### Analysis

- **customers (ε=9.891, σ=1.1)**: Higher epsilon reflects lower noise injection. With σ=1.1,
  the Discriminator gradient updates are only mildly perturbed, allowing the model to learn
  more faithfully but spending more privacy budget. Epsilon ~10 falls in the "non-sensitive
  internal testing" recommended range per the table below.

- **orders (ε=0.685, σ=5.0)**: Good privacy guarantee. With σ=5.0 on 175,000 rows at 3 epochs,
  epsilon stays well below 1.0 — a strong practical DP guarantee. Suitable for data subject to
  moderate regulatory scrutiny.

- **order_items (ε=0.169, σ=10.0)**: Excellent privacy guarantee. The combination of high noise
  (σ=10.0), a large dataset (611,540 source rows), and only 3 epochs yields ε < 0.2 — approaching
  the "very high noise" research/regulatory regime. This is the strongest privacy result of the
  four tables.

- **payments (no DP)**: DP was disabled for this table (`enable_dp=False`). No epsilon is
  consumed. This is appropriate when the table's columns are not considered sensitive PII,
  or when utility requirements outweigh privacy constraints for this specific table.

**Key observation**: Higher noise_multiplier → lower epsilon → stronger privacy guarantee.
This is the expected and correct behavior of DP accounting: more noise injected into the
Discriminator gradients = less information leakage = lower epsilon cost.

## Noise Multiplier Calibration

The following `noise_multiplier` values were calibrated empirically against a 500-row dataset
with 10 training epochs. Because epsilon depends on dataset size, batch size, and number of DP
gradient steps, these values are **specific to this benchmark configuration** and should be
recalibrated for production datasets.

| Target epsilon | noise_multiplier | Rationale |
|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| ~0.1 | 4.00 | Very high noise — strongest privacy, significant quality loss. Research/regulatory scenarios only. |
| ~0.5 | 2.50 | High noise — strong privacy, noticeable quality impact. |
| ~1 | 2.00 | High noise — strong privacy, significant quality loss. Suitable for highly sensitive PII release. |
| ~5 | 0.75 | Moderate noise — balanced trade-off. Suitable for internal analytics on moderately sensitive data. |
| ~10 | 0.55 | Low noise — weaker privacy, better quality. Suitable for non-sensitive synthetic data where utility is paramount. |

**Production-scale calibration note** (from 1M-row load test): At production scale, the same
noise multiplier produces substantially lower epsilon than at the 500-row benchmark scale.
Example: σ=5.0 on 175,000 rows at 3 epochs → ε=0.685, while σ=0.75 on 500 rows at 10 epochs
→ ε≈5.9 (proxy-model). Dataset size is the dominant factor in epsilon accounting — larger
datasets provide more "privacy amplification via subsampling."

## Quality Degradation Analysis

The following observations describe the expected quality degradation curve as epsilon decreases
(stronger privacy):

1. **Vanilla CTGAN** establishes the best-quality baseline. At 10 epochs it already shows
   non-trivial drift; production epochs (300+) would reduce this.
2. **epsilon~10** introduces moderate noise. Mean drifts are typically within 1-2 standard
   deviations of the source column. Categorical distributions are well-preserved.
3. **epsilon~5** increases drift noticeably on numeric columns. Salary columns are more
   affected due to wider absolute ranges amplifying the noise signal.
4. **epsilon~1** produces the strongest common privacy guarantee but at the cost of visible
   distributional distortion. Mean drifts can exceed 2 standard deviations on wide-range
   numeric columns under fast-training conditions.
5. **epsilon~0.5 and epsilon~0.1** represent extreme privacy regimes. Quality degradation is
   severe at these levels under fast-training conditions. Production use at epsilon < 1
   requires substantially more training epochs and hyperparameter tuning.

**Discriminator-level vs proxy-model comparison**: Quality results between the two approaches
will differ because discriminator-level DP directly constrains the GAN training gradient
updates, while the proxy model runs separately from CTGAN. Discriminator-level DP is the only
approach with end-to-end DP guarantees. Proxy-model results are included for historical
comparison only.

## Recommended Epsilon Ranges by Use Case

| Use Case | Recommended Epsilon | Rationale |
|----------|--------------------|-----------|
| External publication / regulatory compliance | 1-2 | Strong privacy guarantee required; quality loss is acceptable. |
| Internal analytics on sensitive PII | 5-8 | Balanced trade-off; distributions are broadly preserved. |
| Non-sensitive internal testing / ML training | 10-20 | Utility is primary concern; privacy is best-effort. |
| Production ML training (quality-first) | No DP (vanilla) | Use only when data sensitivity and regulatory requirements allow. |

## Benchmark Methodology and Limitations

This benchmark is intended as a quick operational health check, not as a production-grade
privacy audit. Key limitations:

- **Low epoch count**: 10 epochs is far below production (300+). Quality metrics here represent
  lower-bound fidelity estimates.
- **Small dataset**: 500 rows. Epsilon accounting is dataset-size-dependent. Production
  datasets will have different epsilon/noise_multiplier relationships.
- **Statistical metrics only**: The benchmark uses mean/stddev drift and cardinality as quality
  proxies. A full production evaluation should also include ML utility metrics and privacy
  attack evaluation.
- **Proxy fallback testing**: The proxy-model section forces the fallback path via an
  intentional first-call failure in `_ProxyForcingWrapper`. This accurately tests the fallback
  behavior but the resulting quality may differ from a native proxy-model deployment.
